## Introduction

Although my family's conversion to Eastern Orthodox Christianity seemed sudden to many in my church community, it was actually the result of a lifelong spiritual journey and years of reflection. My wife and I were deeply involved in the life of our church. We served as youth leaders and volunteered in children's ministries. Maria participated in the worship band, while I was active in men's groups. Because of our involvement, many people viewed us as committed, long-term members of the congregation. To be honest, that is how we viewed ourselves as well.

For that reason, our departure came as a surprise to many people. Some reached out to ask what had happened, while others invited us to dinner to talk about our decision. Many expressed confusion about Orthodoxy and wondered why we would make such a significant change. They did not know the full story behind our departure or the path that had led us there.

This document is my attempt to explain that journey. Our decision to become Orthodox Christians was not made impulsively. Rather, it was the culmination of a lifelong search, especially during the two or three years leading up to our conversion. It grew out of a deeper study of Church history, both Orthodox history and the history of my own tradition, evangelical Protestantism, particularly the Christian and Missionary Alliance. At the same time, I became increasingly convinced that what we were doing on Sundays did not reflect the pattern of worship described in Scripture or practiced by the early Church. Negative experiences with charismatic excesses, spirituality without clear boundaries, and competing interpretations of the Bible also contributed to that conviction.

Eventually, the decision became clear to us, and we knew it was something we needed to do. Even so, I dreaded making it public. I knew it would affect relationships and alter the way many people saw us. Some might feel offended, believing that our departure was a criticism of their faith or worship. Others might worry about our salvation because of the theological differences involved. Those concerns weighed heavily on me because I am not someone who enjoys conflict or confrontation. If possible, I would rather endure discomfort than create tension. In this case, however, remaining silent was no longer an option. I had to bring the decision into the open and accept whatever consequences followed.

Although I expected questions about my conversion to Eastern Orthodoxy, I found it far more difficult than anticipated to explain the deeply personal reasons behind it. I was surprised by how hard it was to answer the simple question, "Why Orthodoxy?" Many people seemed to view it as little more than changing churches, as though it were a matter of preference, like choosing Sheetz over Wawa. But it was much more than that. It involved embracing a new way of life, a new way of seeing and experiencing the Christian faith.

The difficulty was that years of searching could not be condensed into a brief conversation. At times, I wished I could simply point to the words of the Divine Liturgy: "We have seen the true Light. We have received the heavenly Spirit. We have found the true Faith." Those words resonated deeply with my experience, but I knew they could sound dismissive or offensive to sincere Christians who were equally convinced of the truth of their own faith.

I also encountered a variety of assumptions about Orthodoxy. Some people questioned why anyone would embrace so much tradition when, in their view, "all you need is Jesus." Others wondered whether Orthodox churches preached good sermons or taught the Bible. Still others warned that practices such as the veneration of saints and icons were spiritually dangerous. In some cases, people went so far as to suggest that I was abandoning the gospel entirely.

What became clear to me was that most people misunderstood what was actually happening inside me. They often reduced my decision to a theological disagreement, a preference for traditional worship, or an attraction to the aesthetics of Orthodoxy—the "smells and bells." Yet none of those explanations reached the heart of the matter. The challenge was not simply explaining why I became Orthodox. It was explaining the spiritual journey that led me there.

A conversation with my pastor illustrated how easily discussions about my conversion became focused on theology rather than the deeper spiritual journey I was trying to communicate. When I told him that our family would be converting to Eastern Orthodoxy, he responded with kindness, concern, and trust in God's guidance. He assured me that he would be praying for us and reminded me that we would always be welcome back among our church family. I was grateful for his response.

As we began discussing my reasons for leaving, however, the conversation quickly turned toward theology. We talked about differences between Orthodoxy and evangelical Protestantism, including atonement, faith and works, icons, and the saints. These were important topics, but they were not the primary reasons for my decision. What I was struggling to communicate had more to do with my spiritual journey, my experience, and the convictions that had been building over many years.

I soon realized that this was not unique to that conversation. Similar discussions followed with other friends and church leaders. In one case, I met regularly with a man over the course of several weeks, hoping to explain what had led me to Orthodoxy. Yet our conversations repeatedly returned to theological debate. Again and again, I found myself unable to communicate what was happening in my heart.

As a result, I often left those conversations feeling frustrated. I wanted people to understand that my decision was not driven primarily by a desire to settle doctrinal disputes. At the same time, I do not know that I could have explained it much better than I did. Compressing years of searching, questioning, and reflection into a one-hour conversation is difficult. Even now, I sometimes wonder whether I can explain it satisfactorily. Perhaps the gap between the two ways of thinking is simply difficult to bridge in conversation, especially for someone who is not asking the same questions that once drove my own search.

The purpose of this work is simply to tell my story from the beginning. I cannot think of a better way to answer the question, "Why Orthodoxy?" than to explain the path that led me here. My hope is that, by understanding where I have come from, you will better understand how I arrived at where I am today.

I am not trying to convince, persuade, or convert anyone. That is not my goal. I cannot make you see what I saw or feel what I felt, nor can I make you arrive at the same conclusions that I did. All I can do is tell the truth about my journey and allow you to reach your own conclusions.

May God bless the endeavor.

## Early Faith

I grew up in the Christian and Missionary Alliance from the time I was a baby. Because my family did not always have reliable transportation, we often bounced between two different C&MA churches depending on who could give us a ride. Much of our week revolved around church activities, and we relied on others to get us there whenever services or events were taking place.

I also attended a C&MA elementary school, where Bible classes were a regular part of the curriculum. During the summer, I attended a C&MA Bible camp. My grandparents owned a cottage on the campground, and many of my childhood memories are connected to that place. During Family Camp, all of us cousins would sleep together on the back porch during the hot summer nights. We attended children's programs and youth services, often gathering for meetings twice a day throughout the ten days of camp.

From an early age, I was immersed in a revivalist form of Christianity. I loved the camp meetings, the services, and the sense of spiritual excitement that surrounded them. In every direction, my world was shaped by this tradition. Looking back, I would say that I was fully immersed in the phronema of the Christian and Missionary Alliance, even though no one would have used that word at the time.

The C&MA of my childhood felt somewhat old-fashioned by today's standards. Men commonly wore suits and ties to church, while women dressed modestly. Certain activities, such as dancing, playing cards, and drinking alcohol, were discouraged or forbidden in some circles. Although these standards were not observed universally and gradually faded during the 1980s and 1990s, my family—and especially my grandparents' generation—continued to follow them closely.

As I look back, I would describe my religious upbringing as an expression of traditional American Protestantism shaped by church life, revival meetings, camp meetings, and Family Camp. These influences formed the background of my childhood and played a significant role in shaping my understanding of the Christian faith.

It was a world saturated with Scripture and Christian culture. We pledged allegiance to the Christian flag and to the Bible. We sang songs about Scripture, memorized verses, participated in Bible quizzing, and attended Sunday School, Vacation Bible School, and Missions Conferences. Some of my earliest memories are tied to those activities.

I still remember singing songs like, "The B-I-B-L-E, yes, that's the book for me," and "I Will Call Upon the Lord." During one song, all of us kids would kick off our shoes and throw them toward the ceiling. It was a lot of fun to be a child in that environment.

We also played what were called sword drills. We would hold our Bibles in the air while the pastor's wife called out a verse reference. The first person to find the passage, jump to their feet, and read it aloud would win a prize. I was extremely competitive at these games and wanted to win at almost any cost, which probably missed the point of Christian humility. Bible quizzing was similar, except that we competed against students from other churches and had to memorize passages and answer questions about them.

Missions Conferences were another important part of church life. Because the Christian and Missionary Alliance placed such a strong emphasis on missions, these events were always well attended. Missionaries would visit and tell stories about their work around the world. They would bring clothing, artifacts, and photographs from places many of us had never imagined visiting. For a child growing up in rural Pennsylvania, those presentations were fascinating. Many of us dreamed of becoming missionaries ourselves, or at least admired those who did.

On Sunday mornings, while the adults gathered in the sanctuary for worship, we children attended our own junior church service. In many ways, it mirrored what was happening in the main service. We sang hymns and choruses, took an offering, and listened to a sermon or lesson prepared for us.

My mother played the piano, and I often helped by holding the hymnbook and turning the pages. We loved choosing our favorite hymns from the old hymnal—songs like The Old Rugged Cross, Amazing Grace, and Blessed Assurance. I would sit there holding those worn hymnbooks and assume that the songs inside must be incredibly ancient, perhaps even stretching back to the earliest days of Christianity. Of course, I later learned that most of them were written in the nineteenth century, but I had no idea at the time.

Looking back, I realize that our tradition was relatively young. Yet as a child, it never felt that way. I felt as though I belonged to an ancient faith and that the worship we practiced was the same worship Christians had always offered to God. There was a sense of continuity that I could not have explained at the time. I felt connected not only to my parents and grandparents, but also to generations of believers who had come before them. In my mind, that connection stretched all the way back to Jesus Himself.

I remember getting saved at a young age. In the tradition in which I was raised, being saved meant that your sins were forgiven and that you would spend eternity with Christ in heaven. The way one became saved was by repenting of sin and asking Jesus to come into one's heart. There was no official formula, but the prayer usually sounded something like: "Lord Jesus, I know that I am a sinner. I know that You died on the cross for my sins. Please come into my heart, forgive my sins, and take me to heaven to be with You when I die. Amen."

I sincerely prayed that prayer, and I genuinely loved Jesus. I remember feeling deep gratitude and affection for Him because He had died for me. When we sang songs like I Am So Glad That Jesus Loves Me, I sang them from the heart. As far as I knew, my faith was real.

Yet I did not always have assurance that I was saved. On the one hand, I was taught that if I sincerely prayed and meant it, I was saved. On the other hand, whenever someone struggled spiritually or drifted from the faith, people would sometimes ask, "Were they ever really saved?" or "Did they truly mean it?" Those questions unsettled me. If salvation depended on sincere belief, how could I know whether I had been sincere enough?

As a result, I repeatedly prayed the sinner's prayer. I cannot say how many times I asked Jesus to come into my heart. My mother and my Sunday school teachers often reassured me that I did not need to keep doing it, that once was enough. Even so, they were patient and kind, and they would pray with me again whenever I asked because they knew I was struggling with doubt.

Looking back, I think even a child can sense the uncertainty that comes from wondering whether he has believed sincerely enough. I often felt trapped between assurance and doubt. If I truly was saved, then there was no need to pray again. But if I had not been sincere enough the first time, perhaps I was still lost. The tension stayed with me as I grew older, and it became even more significant when I began serving as a youth leader and found myself helping other young people wrestle with the same questions.

I was baptized as a young child, though I can't say for certain how old I was. If I had to guess, I was around nine years old. I remember feeling good about the experience and thinking it was kind of fun. Beyond that, I don't remember much about the day itself. I understood baptism as an outward sign of an inward change, which is how the Christian and Missionary Alliance describes it. There was no belief that baptism itself forgave sins or accomplished anything of that nature. Even so, it was a meaningful experience, a pleasant childhood memory, and another step that strengthened my sense of belonging as a member of the church community.

When I entered my teenage years, I joined the church youth group. This marked a major change in my life because I had reached the age where we were encouraged to make our faith our own. In other words, it was no longer enough to rely on the faith of our parents or grandparents. We were expected to develop a personal faith and a personal relationship with Christ. Looking back, this strikes me as somewhat odd. After all, when I had asked Jesus into my heart as a child, wasn't that already a decision to make the faith my own?

Regardless, I was entering a new world, one I had no idea existed. It was a world filled with fun, chaos, anxiety, emotion, and powerful experiences. Our youth leader poured her heart and soul into the group. She invested countless hours planning activities, encouraging us, and introducing us to Christian music by bands such as DC Talk, Skillet, Relient K, and others.

We regularly held special events at the church. One of the most memorable was the lock-in, where we spent an entire night—and sometimes nearly twenty-four hours—at the church. These events were fun, energetic, and often a little chaotic. We also participated in programs such as the 30 Hour Famine, going without food for thirty hours to raise money and awareness for children suffering from hunger around the world. At times, we ventured into the community for scavenger hunts and other activities that occasionally got us into trouble.

Among all the events and activities, however, the most powerful were the weekend retreats.

Youth group retreats followed a fairly predictable pattern. We would arrive on Friday and stay through Sunday, participating in four main worship services over the course of the weekend. The third service, held on Saturday night, was typically the turning point of the retreat. It was the moment when students were encouraged to make a decision for Christ, come forward to the altar, pray, worship, and recommit their lives to Him.

I experienced one of those moments during a retreat in high school. After a weekend of worship and preaching, I went forward to the altar and broke down in tears. I remember crying so hard that my shoulders shook. One of the youth leaders put his arm around me, prayed for me, and tried to comfort me. At the time, I could not have explained exactly what was happening. I only knew that I felt deeply sorry for my sins. I felt that I had disappointed God, and I wanted His forgiveness, His acceptance, and His love.

Looking back, that experience was the first in a series of moments that another author has called the "rededication cycle," a concept I will discuss later. Even so, I want to be clear that I do not look back on that experience as something insincere or artificial. Whatever else may be said about it, it was real to me. I remember the conviction I felt, the emotions I experienced, and the commitment I made that night to follow Christ.

Although I failed many times afterward and would experience similar moments again in the years to come, I still view that retreat as a significant moment in my life. It marked a sincere commitment to Christ and remains one of the defining spiritual experiences of my youth.

I’ll return to that moment in my life shortly, but first I need to pause and explain the religious framework in which I was spiritually formed. There are a few elements that uniquely shaped my situation and the kind of Christianity I inherited. The first of these is prophecy culture and dispensationalism.

## Prophecy Culture and Dispensationalism

Dispensationalism is an all‑encompassing way of viewing history, where the entire timeline is already mapped out and you’re simply living at a point on that chart—always on the verge of prophecy being fulfilled. Current events are interpreted as signs pointing toward the end times. This framework includes the idea of a rapture, a secret return of Christ that happens before the tribulation begins. It teaches that Israel plays a critical role in the fulfillment of prophecy and that Israel and the Church are distinct entities in God’s plan. It also comes with a whole vocabulary of end‑times concepts—millennium, tribulation, antichrist, mark of the beast—familiar to anyone who grew up with books like Left Behind, The Late Great Planet Earth, or even the Scofield Reference Bible.

I cannot overstate this fact: dispensationalism was the theological air I breathed growing up, even though I didn’t know that word at the time. One side of my family was especially focused on the end times. My grandfather had an entire bookshelf full of prophecy books. We were warned that the rapture could happen at any moment and that we had to be ready for it; otherwise, we could be left behind. This was confusing, because I had invited Jesus into my heart many times and was supposedly safe, yet I lived with a constant fear that I might still be doomed. I was never fully assured that I was sincere enough in my belief. If Jesus was in my heart, why did I have to be ready for the rapture? Wasn’t I already ready? As you will see throughout this document, tensions, contradictions, and crises like these were always present in my faith life.

I remember one afternoon coming home from school to an empty house. My parents had gone shopping and hadn’t returned before the bus dropped me off, and when I realized I was alone, I went into a full panic. I was convinced the rapture had happened and I had been left behind. I went to their rooms to see if there were clothes on the floor where they might have been standing—one of the supposed signs that the rapture had occurred—and turned on the TV to look for reports of car crashes everywhere, planes falling from the sky, and other disasters that would follow millions of Christians suddenly disappearing. I felt sick to my stomach as I imagined the seven years of tribulation I was about to endure. How would I survive? What about the Mark of the Beast? My mind went wild. When my parents finally walked through the door, I burst into tears, completely overwhelmed. They laughed, probably thinking it looked cute from their perspective, and maybe because they had felt the same fear themselves when they were young. But I remember it not being funny at all. It had never even occurred to me to check whether their car was in the driveway or if they had simply gone out.

We were warned constantly about the Mark of the Beast. We understood the Antichrist to be a charismatic figure—probably a politician—who would establish a new world order and a new economy in which people would need to receive some kind of barcode-like mark on their forehead or right hand in order to buy or sell anything. We were convinced the Antichrist was already alive and on the scene, simply waiting to reveal himself, and we believed the technology was already in place to implement the Mark of the Beast globally. It could be hidden in barcodes, credit cards, or some other emerging technology. It was terrifying, because receiving the Mark meant eternal damnation with no possibility of forgiveness. Although the Bible says the only unforgivable sin is blasphemy against the Holy Spirit, we were taught that after the rapture God had essentially abandoned the world, leaving no chance for repentance. In that context, the Mark of the Beast became an unforgivable sin that would send you straight to hell, and there was nothing you could do to remove it. We watched movies like A Thief in the Night that scared the living daylights out of me, showing people who had received the Mark realizing what they had done and being filled with terror at the doom awaiting them at the end of the tribulation. Even the number 666 itself was treated as evil, something you shouldn’t say, and encountering it in daily life was considered an ill omen.

We watched TV shows about a phenomenon called the Bible Code—the idea that God had embedded secret messages in Scripture that could be uncovered by following certain patterns, such as skipping every few letters or tracing crossword‑like or acrostic sequences through the text. The methods for finding these codes were never clear: sometimes they were supposedly hidden in the original Greek manuscripts, other times they appeared in the English Bible. Names like Winston Churchill, Napoleon, and John F. Kennedy; events like September 11, 2001 or the end of World War II; and phrases like “behold the blood of Jesus” were all said to appear through the use of the Bible Code. Secret numbers were scattered everywhere—7, 12, 40, 144,000, 666—and each was believed to carry a mystical meaning we were expected to decipher. It always felt like there was a deeper layer of hidden knowledge behind the plain reading of Scripture. Another thing that bothered me, even as a child, was that to learn more about the Bible Code you had to buy the author’s book or video series. That drove me crazy, because it felt like we needed this knowledge, and it didn’t seem fair that we couldn’t access it simply because we didn’t have the money.

We watched television prophets like Jack Van Impe, who explained the end times in great detail and offered books and video series to help us prepare. Growing up, I was only allowed to stay up late on Saturday nights if I watched Jack Van Impe Presents with my parents. So there I would be, watching Jack Van Impe connect current events and newspaper headlines to the Book of Revelation, convinced that every new development in the world was a sign that the end was near. He always had a big smile on his face, as if to say, “Yes, it’s almost here. Yes, Lord, come quickly,” and his wife Rexella would sit beside him, encouraging it all. The strange thing was that so much of his joy and excitement came from terrible situations in the world—war, famine, terrorist attacks in Israel or elsewhere in the Middle East, Russia being aggressive. These were all things that were bad for the world, yet he looked on them with glee because he believed they were ushering in the rapture. I was completely engrossed in this. I was excited, and I was all in for everything he preached. Again, I wished we had the money to buy the video series and books, but we didn’t, and that made me feel like we were missing out on the “extra good” prophecy. And yet, whenever I wondered when the actual rapture was going to occur, it always seemed imminent—and it never happened.

Let me give you a strange example of the absurdity of dispensationalist theology. I was told as a child that I would not have to worry about dying because Christ was going to return before I did. I was one of the “lucky” ones, born when I was, because if you calculated from 1948, a generation was considered only 60 or 70 years. That meant that someone born in the 1980s would not yet be in their thirties when the rapture happened. The reason 1948 mattered was that the Bible says a generation would not pass away before all these things were accomplished, and dispensationalists interpreted this verse—out of context—to mean the generation alive at the founding of the modern state of Israel in 1948. If that generation could not pass away before Christ returned, then I was basically guaranteed to be raptured before ever having to worry about dying. I believed I was immortal. It was strange, and it made coming to grips with my own mortality later in life more difficult than it should have been.
